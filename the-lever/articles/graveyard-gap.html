The Graveyard Gap: knowing what to do and actually doing it are $200K apart

Every restaurant owner knows they should be doing email marketing, Google Ads, and review responses. Almost none are. That gap has a price tag.

K Kyle
March 7, 2026 Issue No. 009

I talk to hundreds of restaurant owners a year. And here’s the thing that keeps me up at night: they all know what they should be doing.

Every single one. Ask any restaurant owner if they should be sending email newsletters to their customer list. They’ll say yes. Ask if they should be responding to Google reviews. Yes. Running targeted Google Ads for their neighborhood? Yes. Posting consistently on social media? Yes, yes, yes.

Now ask them if they’re actually doing any of it.

The silence is deafening.

The gap that eats restaurants alive

I started calling it the Graveyard Gap about six months ago, and the name stuck because it’s morbidly accurate. It’s the space between knowing what to do and actually doing it. And it’s not a small space. It’s not a crack in the sidewalk. It’s a canyon.

$200K
average annual revenue left on the table

That number comes from our own analysis across the restaurants we’ve worked with over the past two years. When we add up the revenue from email campaigns that never got sent, the Google traffic that never got captured, the repeat customers who never got a follow-up — it averages out to roughly $200,000 per year in unrealized revenue. Per restaurant.

Not theoretical revenue. Revenue that comparable restaurants in comparable markets are capturing — because they’re actually doing the things everyone knows they should do.

“The graveyard of good intentions is full of restaurant owners who meant to send that email.”

Why knowing isn’t the problem

The restaurant industry has an information surplus and an execution deficit. There are more courses, guides, YouTube videos, and Instagram reels about restaurant marketing than any human could consume in a lifetime. The information is free. It’s everywhere. It’s not the bottleneck.

The bottleneck is Tuesday at 2 PM when the lunch rush just ended, the dishwasher called in sick, a supplier shorted you on produce, and you’re supposed to sit down and write a “compelling email newsletter” for your customer list. That newsletter doesn’t get written. Not because you don’t know how. Because you’re exhausted, overwhelmed, and there are fourteen things that feel more urgent.

Multiply that by every week, for every marketing task, across a year. That’s the Graveyard Gap.

The answer isn’t more willpower

Here’s the part most marketing advice gets catastrophically wrong: it assumes the problem is motivation. “You just need to prioritize it.” “Make time for marketing.” “It’s only 30 minutes a day.”

With respect: shut up. Nobody who’s running a restaurant with a sick dishwasher and a shorted produce order needs a lecture on prioritization. They need systems.

Systems that run without willpower. Automated workflows that fire on schedule whether you remembered to sit down at 2 PM or not. Review response templates that go out automatically. Email sequences triggered by customer behavior, not by you remembering to click “send.” Google Business Profile posts that schedule a week in advance during the one calm hour you have on Sunday morning.

What closing the gap actually looks like

The restaurants that close the Graveyard Gap don’t do it by becoming better at marketing. They do it by building systems that remove them from the equation.

Automated review responses — not generic ones, but templated responses with personalization that go out within 24 hours of every review. Setup time: 45 minutes. Ongoing effort: zero.

Triggered email sequences — a “we miss you” email at 30 days since last visit, a birthday offer, a new-menu-item announcement. Setup time: 2 hours. Ongoing effort: one new email per month.

Scheduled GBP posts — batch-create four posts on Sunday, schedule them for the week. Setup time: 20 minutes/week. Ongoing effort: 20 minutes/week. That’s it.

Auto-reporting dashboards — a weekly email that tells you your numbers without you having to log into anything. Setup time: 1 hour. Ongoing effort: reading one email.

None of this is revolutionary. None of it requires a marketing degree. But the difference between having these systems and not having them?

About $200K a year.

The graveyard is full. The question is whether you’re going to keep visiting it every week with good intentions, or whether you’re going to build the systems that make your intentions irrelevant.

The gap isn’t going to close itself. But it doesn’t have to be you who closes it. It just has to be a system.
