Experiment

We let AI write 47 ad variations for a burger restaurant. Three beat everything we’d ever made by hand.

Most of the AI-generated ads were garbage. But the three that worked? They worked better than anything we’d written in two years.

L The Lab
March 7, 2026 Issue No. 009

Here’s the experiment: take one burger restaurant, give AI every piece of information about the business — menu, vibe, reviews, location, target audience — and ask it to generate 47 Facebook and Instagram ad variations. Then run all of them against the restaurant’s best-performing human-written ads.

We expected AI to lose. We were wrong. Sort of.

The 47-ad gauntlet

Let’s be honest about the full picture. Of the 47 ad variations AI produced, most were mediocre to bad. About 30 of them read like a committee wrote them — technically correct, emotionally flat, the kind of copy that makes you scroll past without a second thought. Another 14 were aggressively weird in ways that didn’t land: one compared eating a burger to “a sunset for your mouth.” No.

But three of them? Three of them were something else entirely.

The three that broke the pattern

Winner #1 had a hook we never would have written: “You’ve driven past this burger 412 times.” It used the restaurant’s location on a busy commuter road as the entire angle. We’d never thought about the drive-past traffic as a hook. The AI did. 34% higher CTR than our best human ad.

Winner #2 went personal in a way that felt risky: “Your Tuesday deserves a better burger than your Tuesday usually gets.” It targeted the specific day of the week when the restaurant was slowest. We wouldn’t have run a Tuesday-specific ad. AI didn’t care about our assumptions. 51% higher CTR.

Winner #3 was the strangest. The headline was: “We asked our grill cook what makes our burger different. He said ‘I dunno, the fire, probably.’” Anti-marketing. Self-deprecating. Real. 67% higher CTR than anything we’d ever run for this restaurant.

“The AI didn’t write better ads. It wrote weirder ads. Turns out, weird works.”

The pattern behind the winners

When we analyzed why those three worked, the answer was uncomfortable for anyone who writes ads for a living (including us). The winning AI ads shared one trait: they tried angles that a professional copywriter would self-edit out.

A human writer would never pitch the “you’ve driven past this 412 times” angle because it doesn’t sound like “good copy.” A human writer would never run a Tuesday-specific ad because it feels too narrow. A human writer would never use a quote from a grill cook saying “I dunno” because it sounds unprofessional.

AI has no professional ego. It doesn’t self-edit based on what “should” work. It just generates options. And when you generate 47 options without the filter of “what would a client approve,” you stumble into angles that are too weird, too specific, too honest — and sometimes, exactly right.

What this means for your ad budget

We’re not saying fire your copywriter. The AI’s hit rate was 3 out of 47 — about 6%. A good human writer probably hits at 30–40%. But here’s the math that matters: generating 47 AI variations took 12 minutes. Writing 10 human variations took 4 hours.

The play isn’t AI or human. It’s AI and human. Use AI to generate volume — the weird, the unexpected, the angles you’d never try. Then use human judgment to pick the three that might actually work. Test them. Let the data decide.

That’s what we did. And the grill cook’s burger sold out that week.

“I dunno, the fire, probably.” Best ad we never wrote.
